I have spent most of my time in privacy tech. Part of me chose this field because of a quiet conviction that something has gone wrong. Not with technology itself, but with the culture it grew out of and the one it helped reinforce.

Technology amplifies what is already there, but it also introduces capabilities that did not exist before. The loneliness, the erosion of community, the concentration of economic power in fewer and fewer hands were already in motion before smartphones existed. A culture that measures progress in terms of growth and engagement was always going to produce tools that maximize growth and engagement, and a culture that treats privacy as a barrier to commerce was always going to build infrastructure that collects everything it can. The harms that followed were not unforeseen accidents. For many of the people building these systems, they were acceptable trade-offs. Mark Fisher called this capitalist realism: the current system becomes so total that alternatives fall outside the horizon of what can be thought, not suppressed but simply unimaginable [@Fisher2009]. But algorithmic recommendation at planetary scale, behavioral prediction as a tradable asset, and generative systems that produce content faster than humans can evaluate it are not continuations of older dynamics. They are new capabilities, deployed faster than any serious attempt to evaluate them. The cultural substrate explains why they were built. It does not exhaust what they did.

The internet could have been built differently, but it wasn't. And we're paying for it in ways that are increasingly hard to ignore.

These are things I keep coming back to. Not breaking news, not exhaustive, just a list I needed to write down.

## Attention

The modern smartphone is, among other things, a slot machine you carry in your pocket. Variable reward schedules are baked into every notification, every pull-to-refresh, every infinite scroll. The same mechanism that makes gambling addictive. None of this is accidental, it is the product of deliberate engineering.

Gloria Mark spent years at UC Irvine measuring how long people focus on a task before switching. In 2004, the average was about two and a half minutes. By 2020, it had dropped to 47 seconds [@GloriaMark]. Whether you attribute this to smartphones specifically or the broader fragmentation of digital life, the trend is clear and it mirrors the rise of the attention economy almost exactly.

What's more troubling than the raw numbers is what we know about recovery. Every time you pick up your phone mid-task, the quality of focus you return with is measurably worse. It takes roughly 20 minutes of uninterrupted work to get back to the level of concentration you had before the interruption [@GloriaMark]. Jonathan Crary described the broader logic in 24/7: late capitalism was already systematically eliminating the boundaries between work and rest, attention and recovery, until time outside of productivity became hard to defend [@Crary2013]. The smartphone completed that project.

## Mental health

Rates of anxiety, depression, and self-harm among teenagers started rising around 2012, and the trend appears across the UK, Canada, Australia, and the US. That timing lines up with the global adoption of smartphones and social platforms, and Jonathan Haidt and Jean Twenge have spent years arguing the connection is causal [@AnxiousGeneration] [@Twenge2018].

This is the most contested section of the case. Amy Orben, Andrew Przybylski, and Candice Odgers have pushed back from several directions: the average effect of social media use on wellbeing is small once you control for prior conditions, the underlying datasets often confound time online with kinds of use that vary widely in their effects, and the cross-country evidence is less consistent than the strongest claims suggest [@Orben2019]. Odgers in particular has argued that the available studies do not support the causal weight Haidt places on them, and that focusing on screens risks crowding out attention to known drivers of adolescent distress like poverty, instability, and disrupted services [@Odgers2020]. The teen mental health crisis is real. The role of smartphones in it is genuinely uncertain.

The qualitative arguments do not rest entirely on the disputed quantitative evidence. Social comparison now operates at a scale and speed that was previously impossible. Cyberbullying does not end at the school gate. Sleep is disrupted by screens and notification anxiety. Hours spent watching other people's curated lives is plausibly not good for how you feel about your own. For teenage girls especially, the visual and social nature of Instagram-era platforms appears to have been particularly harmful in the studies that find effects [@Twenge2018]. None of this requires the strongest version of Haidt's argument to be true.

Byung-Chul Han described the cultural substrate in The Burnout Society: a society that replaced external discipline with internal pressure to achieve, until the subject exhausts itself from the inside [@Han2015]. The platforms did not create that pressure, but they gave it a new surface to operate on, regardless of how the causal question is eventually resolved.

## Loneliness

The share of Americans who said they had no close friends went from 3% in 1990 to 12% in 2021 [@FriendshipDecline]. In 2023, the US Surgeon General declared loneliness and social isolation a public health crisis, with health effects he compared to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day [@SurgeonGeneralLoneliness].

This has happened during the period of maximum digital connectivity. More followers, more group chats, more contacts, but fewer people you can actually call when something goes wrong. The mechanism is not hard to understand: online interaction is mostly passive and asymmetric, you consume other people's content and occasionally react, and that does not build the kind of reciprocal, face-to-face relationship that actually reduces loneliness. Time on platforms is time not spent on the things that matter.

The downstream effects are harder to measure but worth noting. Dating apps introduced the paradox of choice at a very large scale: a theoretically infinite supply of potential partners, which in practice correlates with less commitment, shorter relationships, and people taking longer to settle down [@TwengeSexualFrequency]. The share of young men under 30 reporting no sexual activity in the past year roughly doubled between 2008 and 2018. Birth rates have been declining across the developed world for decades for reasons that long predate smartphones, but starting a family also requires a network around you: people who have done it, a community that makes it feel possible, relationships deep enough to provide real support. That infrastructure is harder to build when the social fabric has thinned. Whether that link is decisive is hard to measure, but it is not nothing.

## Surveillance

This one I care about professionally, so I will try to be precise rather than dramatic.

Surveillance capitalism is Shoshana Zuboff's term for the business model of converting behavioral data into predictive products sold to third parties [@Zuboff2019]. It is not a conspiracy theory. It is a documented industry practice. Every search, every click, every pause, the content of messages on platforms that scan them, precise location history, emotional state inferred from typing speed and scroll behavior. All of this is used to build models of you that are sold to advertisers, insurers, employers, and governments, often without your knowledge and almost always without real consent.

The Cambridge Analytica case showed what happens when that data reaches people with political intent, but the routine data broker market, which operates with almost no regulation in most countries, is a far larger and less visible problem.

What makes this particularly bad is the consent architecture. "You agreed to the terms of service" is the legal shield, but the terms are written to be impossible to understand, and the alternative to agreeing is exclusion from services that have become essential. That is not consent in any real sense.

## Disinformation

A 2018 study published in Science tracked the spread of true and false news on Twitter over ten years and found that false stories spread faster, further, and more widely than true ones, and that this was driven by people, not bots [@Vosoughi2018]. False news was 70% more likely to be retweeted.

The reason is not complicated: novelty and emotional reaction drive sharing, and false stories tend to be more surprising and more provocative than accurate ones. The algorithms that decide what to show you are optimized for engagement, which means they are structurally biased toward content that triggers a reaction, regardless of whether it is true.

The effects are measurable. COVID misinformation affected vaccination rates in ways that researchers have been able to quantify. Climate denial has been systematically amplified. A 2019 study from the Oxford Internet Institute covering 70 countries found organized social media manipulation in all of them, with state-sponsored actors using platforms to spread false information and suppress political opposition [@GlobalDisinformation].

## AI

The other problems in this list mostly share a common origin: platforms designed to maximize engagement at the expense of everything else. AI is different in kind, not just degree.

The concentration of power in a few tech companies was already a problem. A small number of platforms controlled most of the digital infrastructure, the data, and the attention. AI compounds that concentration because it adds a barrier that did not exist before. The Stanford AI Index reported that in 2023, industry produced 51 notable machine learning models, against 15 from academia, a gap that did not exist a decade ago [@StanfordAIIndex2024]. Training costs for frontier models have moved from millions to hundreds of millions of dollars in a few years, with GPT-4 estimated at around $78 million and Gemini Ultra at $191 million in compute alone, putting them structurally out of reach for public institutions and most startups [@StanfordAIIndex2024]. The data advantage accumulated over the past two decades feeds directly into model quality [@AINow2023]. Open weight releases from Meta, DeepSeek, and others complicate the picture, and the cost curve will keep moving, but the structural point holds. The companies that won the platform era are best positioned to win the AI era because the inputs are the same: data, compute, distribution, talent.

What is harder to fix is the accountability gap. Decisions with large societal implications, about what models optimize for, what data they were trained on, what they are deployed to do, are being made inside private companies with minimal external oversight and strong incentives to move fast. The EU's AI Act is the most serious regulatory attempt so far, but it was written while the technology was moving, and parts of it were outdated before it passed. The deeper problem is that the governance frameworks we have, built for products and services, do not map cleanly onto systems whose behavior emerges from training rather than design. You cannot regulate a model the way you regulate a feature, and the field has not yet built the tools to do something better.

There is something uncomfortable about a world where the infrastructure of intelligence itself is controlled by a small number of corporations. It is a different kind of problem than a bad product decision, and the surface area for fixing it is smaller than it was for the platforms.

---

None of this is to say technology is net negative, and the version we got was a choice and not an inevitability.

And there is something moving underneath that does not get much attention. The W3C and a broader open-source community have been building standards and protocols that point toward a healthier web, one closer to what the internet was supposed to be: decentralized, user-controlled, interoperable. ActivityPub, the protocol behind Mastodon and the fediverse, is a small but real example of this. These efforts are slow and often invisible compared to the products that dominate the market, but they are there.

In cryptography, protocols are maturing that could change what privacy means in practice. Zero-knowledge proofs and related techniques make it possible to verify things about data without revealing the data itself. The clearest near-term example is age verification online. Governments in the UK, Australia, and parts of the EU are now requiring it, and most current implementations involve uploading a passport or driver's license to a third party, which is a privacy disaster waiting to leak. Zero-knowledge proofs let you prove you are over 18 from a credential you already hold, without revealing the document, your name, or anything else on it. Projects like Self, which uses zero-knowledge proofs over biometric passport chips to verify age and nationality without revealing identity, are already deployed in production [@SelfProtocol]. Multi-party computation and homomorphic encryption push the envelope further, allowing computation on data that is never decrypted, though those are earlier in the path from research to real use.

The academic community is also taking these issues more seriously. Work like Haidt's is now part of a real literature, debated and built upon. The psychological effects of the attention economy, on children especially, are no longer a fringe concern.

And governments are starting to act. Imperfectly, often too slowly, sometimes in the wrong direction. But the EU's Digital Services Act, GDPR enforcement, age verification laws, and growing antitrust pressure on large platforms are signs that the political will to push back is building. None of this is enough on its own. But the direction is at least being contested.

The costs of the choices we made are showing up clearly in the data. So are the first signs that some people decided not to accept them.
